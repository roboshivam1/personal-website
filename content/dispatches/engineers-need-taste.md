Walk into most engineering exhibitions and you’ll notice something curious. The projects work. They solve the problem. They have the right components and the right code.

But very few are memorable.

Not because they lack technical skill, but because they lack taste.

Engineering education teaches us to optimize for performance, reliability, and efficiency. It rarely teaches us to ask whether something feels beautiful, whether the enclosure is elegant, whether the interface invites interaction, or whether every detail feels intentional.

The products that stay with us-a Leica camera, a Koenigsegg, an Apple laptop-aren’t remembered for specifications alone. They are remembered because engineering and design were treated as inseparable.

Good taste isn’t decoration. It’s discipline. It’s caring about the details most people never consciously notice.

Perhaps that’s why I spend as much time studying typography, industrial design, and automotive engineering as I do writing code. None of those things directly make me a better programmer. But together, they make me a better builder.

Because in the end, engineering isn’t just about making something work.

It’s about making something worth remembering.
